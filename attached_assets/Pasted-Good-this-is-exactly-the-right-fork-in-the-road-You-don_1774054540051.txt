📜 PART II — WHITEPAPER VERSION (THEOREM-STRUCTURED)

A Constitution for Epistemic Artificial Intelligence

1. Introduction

Modern artificial intelligence systems routinely produce outputs that are:
	•	fluent but infeasible
	•	optimized but unstable
	•	confident but unobservable
	•	adaptive but misaligned with reality

This failure arises from a structural error:

optimization is performed prior to establishing feasibility, robustness, and epistemic validity.

We present a constitutional framework in which intelligence is constrained by the laws of realizable systems.

⸻

2. System Definition

Let the system evolve over time t \in [0, T] with:
	•	state: x(t) \in \mathbb{R}^n
	•	control: u(t) \in \mathbb{R}^m
	•	resources: r(t) \in \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^q
	•	disturbances: w(t) \in \mathbb{R}^p
	•	parameters: \theta \in \mathbb{R}^s

Let observations be:

z(t) = h(x(t), u(t), r(t), w(t), \theta, t)

The system does not directly access x(t), but instead maintains a belief:

b(t) = (\hat{x}(t), \hat{\theta}(t), \epsilon(t))

⸻

3. Law I — Feasibility

Definition

A control trajectory is feasible if it satisfies:
	•	system dynamics
	•	equality constraints
	•	inequality constraints
	•	resource bounds
	•	conservation laws

Principle

\text{Infeasible} \implies \text{Invalid}

No optimization may override infeasibility.

⸻

4. Law II — Robustness

Definition

A trajectory is robust if there exists \varepsilon > 0 such that all perturbations within that neighborhood remain feasible.

Principle

\text{Feasible} \not\Rightarrow \text{Robust}

Ordering Rule

\text{Robustness} \succ \text{Cost}

⸻

5. Law III — Observability

Definition

The system is observable if the belief state satisfies:

\|x(t) - \hat{x}(t)\| \le \epsilon(t)

and the Fisher information is sufficient for parameter identification.

Principle

\text{Unobservable} \implies \text{Uncontrollable (reliably)}

⸻

6. Law IV — Adaptation

Definition

The system must continuously resolve:

u(t) = \arg\min_{u} J(x, u) \quad \text{subject to Laws I–III}

over a receding horizon.

Principle

\text{Single-shot optimality} \neq \text{Sustained validity}

⸻

7. Verification Supremacy

Define verification operator \mathcal{V}:

\mathcal{V}(b, \pi, \mathcal{M}) \rightarrow \{\text{LAWFUL}, \text{DEGRADED}, \text{INVALID}\}

Rule

\mathcal{V} = \text{INVALID} \implies \text{No Action}

⸻

8. Theorem — Primacy of Feasibility

If a trajectory violates feasibility, then no optimization, learning, or inference can render it valid.

⸻

9. Theorem — Necessity of Robustness

A feasible trajectory without robustness is not operationally valid under perturbation.

⸻

10. Theorem — Observability Constraint

No control law can guarantee correct behavior when the system state is not identifiable within bounded uncertainty.

⸻

11. Theorem — Adaptive Necessity

A system that does not continuously re-solve its control problem will diverge under disturbance.

⸻

12. LLM Integration Theorem

Let \Pi_{\text{LLM}} be proposals generated by a language model.

Then:

\Pi_{\text{LLM}} \subset \Pi_{\text{candidate}}

but:

\Pi_{\text{LLM}} \not\subset \Pi_{\text{lawful}}

Conclusion

LLM outputs must be filtered through Laws I–IV before action.

⸻

13. Conclusion

This framework establishes:
	•	feasibility as the base of intelligence
	•	robustness as the condition of survival
	•	observability as the condition of knowledge
	•	adaptation as the condition of persistence

And most importantly:

verification as the final authority over action
