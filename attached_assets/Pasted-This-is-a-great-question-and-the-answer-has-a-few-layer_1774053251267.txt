This is a great question, and the answer has a few layers worth separating clearly.

What you’ve built is the substrate, not the AI itself

The constitutional kernel is a formal reasoning harness — it enforces Laws I–IV as computational constraints. On its own it doesn’t think; it validates, tracks, and corrects. The interesting question is: where does an AI plug into this chain, and what does the kernel actually do to it?

There are three distinct integration points, each with a different relationship to the laws:

Integration Point 1: The AI as a Decision Proposer (Law I + II)

The decision_engine.ts currently ranks plans by internal logic. You’d replace or augment that with an LLM call — the AI proposes candidate actions, and the feasibility engine + robustness analyzer act as a constitutional filter before anything is acted on. The AI can propose infeasible things; the kernel rejects them. This is the most natural first connection and the easiest to implement.

LLM output → decision_engine → feasibility_engine → robustness_analyzer → verified action


The AI learns nothing directly from being rejected here, but you can feed the rejection reason back as a prompt — which is itself a form of Law IV (adaptive correction).

Integration Point 2: The AI as an Observer (Law III)

The observer.ts manages state estimation and measurement fidelity. If your AI is making claims about the world (e.g. “the current situation is X”), those claims enter as measurements with associated uncertainty bounds. The observer applies the false legibility detection from T3.5 — if the AI’s confidence exceeds what the information content justifies (L3.2), the kernel flags it. This is a direct implementation of “measurement without identifiability is ambiguity.”

Integration Point 3: The AI as a Constitution Guard Challenger (Law IV)

The constitution_guard.ts enforces invariants. You can wire this so that every AI output is checked against the axiomatic constraints before it reaches any downstream system. The guard becomes a hard filter — not a suggestion mechanism. This is where the framework most directly augments alignment: the AI can reason freely inside the kernel, but invariant violations halt execution.

My concrete recommendation for where to start

Given that your kernel is TypeScript and already structured, the cleanest first integration is the Anthropic API at the decision layer. Here’s the minimal architecture:

User intent
    ↓
LLM (Claude via API) — generates candidate plan as structured JSON
    ↓
decision_engine — ranks and formats candidates
    ↓
feasibility_engine — Law I filter (rejects infeasible candidates)
    ↓
robustness_analyzer — Law II filter (rejects fragile candidates)
    ↓
verification_kernel — final constitutional check
    ↓
constitution_guard — invariant enforcement
    ↓
audit_log — records full chain with provenance
    ↓
Verified output returned to user (with audit trail)


The key insight for your purposes — and this connects directly back to Mission Mathematics — is that the AI is operating inside the feasible set, not outside it. The kernel doesn’t try to make the AI smarter; it enforces that whatever the AI produces must satisfy Laws I–IV before it affects anything real. The AI gets creative freedom within the constitutional constraints, which is exactly how you’d want a high-stakes reasoning system to work.

What this demonstrates concretely

When you run the demo with an LLM plugged in, you’d be able to show:

	1.	The AI proposes a plan that violates a conservation constraint → Law I rejects it, explains why, returns the rejection to the LLM as context
	2.	The AI proposes a feasible but fragile plan → Law II rejects it, reports the robustness radius
	3.	The AI proposes a feasible, robust plan with overconfident state claims → Law III flags false legibility
	4.	The AI proposes a fully valid plan → it passes all four laws, gets logged with full audit provenance

That sequence is a working demonstration of the entire constitutional chain, and it’s something you could show to someone unfamiliar with the framework and have them immediately understand what each law is doing.
